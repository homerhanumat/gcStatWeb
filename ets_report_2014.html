Introduction

The ETS

  • ETS Proficiency Profile
  • Given annually to seniors at 252 four-year institutions
  • Overall scaled score 400-500
  • Four scaled subscores (100-125), in these areas:
    • Mathematics
    • Reading
    • Writing
    • Critical Thinking

Sample Questions Here

In addition to scaled scores, the Profile attempts to assess proficiency in three areas:

  • Reading and Critical Thinking
  • Writing
  • Mathematics

Assessment is made at each of three levels (Level 1,2 and 3) in each area.

Methods

Adjust for Academic Preparation!

ETS performance determined largely by academic preparation (\(R^2 =\) 0.53):

Data Munging

For many of our questions, we would like to correct for differences in incoming academic preparation.

Accordingly, we merged the ETS data with a GC dataset that gives:

  • College name of record

  • ACT scores (composite and subscores)

  • actual graduation year

  • actual graduation major(s). (ETS asks students to pick from several "types" of major)

Data Munging

Merging was not easy!

  • Students often entered wrong ID number

  • On ETS, students:
    • use nicknames
    • use new married name
    • spell name differently than GC does
    • don't graduate when they think they will
    • don't graduate at all

With a bit of computer programming, we ended up matching 738 of the 800 ETS students with their GC data.

Linear Models

Example (in question about sex making difference in Total ETS score):

\[Total = \alpha + \beta_0 \times ACT = \beta_1 \times Female + \epsilon\]

  • \(\alpha\) represents the predicted score for a student of average composite ACT and unknown sex.
  • \(\beta_0\) tells how much your predicted score increases for each one-point increase in ACT Composite score.
  • \(\beta_1\) represents the difference in score that is predicted for a female relative to a person with same ACT whose sex is not known.
  • \(\epsilon\) is chance error.
  • Note: we will be using "sum contrasts", not "treatment contrasts."

Proficiency: Crticial Thinking

This is Level 3 of the Reading proficiency levels. To be proficient at this level student must be able to:

  • evaluate competing causal explanations
  • evaluate hypotheses for consistency with known facts
  • determine the relevance of information for evaluating an argument or conclusion
  • determine whether an artistic interpretation is supported by evidence contained in a work
  • evaluate the appropriateness of procedures for investigating a question of causation
  • evaluate data for consistency with known facts, hypotheses or methods
  • recognize flaws and inconsistencies in an argument

Conclusion

Future Data Collection

Proctor the ETS more carefully:

  • Make sure student enter correct ID

  • guard against fraud

Cautions

  • Sometimes our results are "statistically significant"
  • But we are guilty of some "data snooping"
  • Also, we made many, many comparisons, obtained many P-values
  • These need to be adjusted for multiple comparisons
  • Probably won't be able to draw firm conclusions right now
  • (except that: for the most part, nothing is going on!)
  • Need to "watch" certain patterns
  • Gather more data over the years, see if "watched" patterns persist

Confounding (example)

Students have no vested interest in doing well on the ETS.

If students differ by major in the amount of effort they choose to put forth …

… then differences in performance by major could be due to differences in "student academic culture", not growth in academic ability.

Assessment: a Black Hole?

May need 6-8 more years before we can be sure of "suspected" patterns …

… and by then, underlying conditions at GC may have changed!

So: how do we use the data to improve our programs?
